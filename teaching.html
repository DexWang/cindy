Teaching

I was born in the hometown of Confucius, China’s greatest philosopher and educator, so I have always appreciated the work of a teacher. Some of my close family members are school teachers and college professors.  While growing up, I watched how they interact with their students and develop close teacher-student relationships. My teaching philosophy holds that if the teacher pays careful attention and displays devotion, every student can succeed. Teaching is not limited to the classroom or bounded by the teaching schedule. It entails study, careful preparation of materials, thoughtful assignments, and authentic interaction with students outside of class.

My teaching style has evolved over the last four years at Oregon through direct experience of developing curricula and teaching courses, and by using the feedback I receive to improve. My approach centers on involving students in actively engaging with the material through discussion, cases, group exercises, and written assignments. I believe that students need this active engagement to not only grasp the core ideas of a class, but to understand how these ideas apply to both the business world and their current lives.

In addition to attending to each student’s performance in class, I work to facilitate the learning process. I send each one of my students an individual survey before class begins every term. I ask them to list what they want to learn most from this class and which evaluation formats they prefer. I design my class based on the teaching guidelines from the department but customize it according to students’ needs. When I deliver my lectures, I incorporate real life situations and examples based on my own working experience and encourage students to think beyond the text book and other learning materials.  Realizing that students struggle to understand abstract and intellectual concepts, I use vivid in-class activities to increase their engagement and interest in learning. For example, in my introduction to marketing’s classic four P’s (Product, Price, Place, Promotion), I ask students to get into groups and work on a lemonade stand project that could be implemented the coming weekend to raise funds for the local elementary school. With real life scenarios, students are more thorough in planning and implementing the four P’s. My students tell me this is a very effective way for them to understand, apply and remember these concepts.

I share my passion for marketing with my students. For example, at the beginning of the term, one of my students, Alex Costello, found marketing boring and regularly arrived for class late and left early.. When I sat down with him, he told me that he was fascinated by computer science and had no interest at all in marketing. During our one-hour conversation, I explained to him how computer science knowledge could support quantitative analysis of Big Data to impact companies, consumers, and the market. He became inspired and started to see the value of the field of marketing. He ended up being one of the most actively participating and best performing students in class.  In his course evaluation, he wrote: "Prof. Wang was awesome. I'm usually skeptical of foreign teachers because of the language barrier, but Cindy was a total exception to this. She was very clear, smart, and passionate. Honestly, I think her biggest strength was the fact she cared. So many professors don't care, but Cindy really wanted to make an impact on her students -- and it showed.".

As a teacher, I make myself very accessible both in and outside of the classroom. I believe every student, regardless of their performance in class, deserves a teacher’s full attention.  In every class that I teach, I devote at least one half hours outside of class to meet with each of my students individually. This takes considerable time for a large class, but I find it to be worthwhile. As one student wrote in his evaluation: "Cindy did an excellent job getting to know students and I give her kudos for her ability to get all students introduced to her one-on-one during office hours. I think that introducing yourself to your professor is something important that most students overlook and I give her all the credit for requiring students to do so. I also think that she keeps up to date material in her slides and really enables class time interaction with her "question for you" slides and her in-class interactive activities."

In short, I make myself approachable and accessible whenever and however students need me. I create a personal relationship with students so that they feel comfortable coming to me for help with the course as well as other problems they encounter in life.  I value this trust.
